\begin{abstract}

Traditional automated mushroom identification is primarily a unimodal image classification task, which most systems predicting only species labels and numerical confidence scores from a single image. However, such a single modality approaches, lack of ecological grounding, language based reasoning, and interpretable outputs,limiting their practical utilities in real world contexts, where misidentifying mushroom species can result in severe health consequences. 
In this study, we proposes a unified multimodal mushroom classification framework centered around a four stage pipeline: (1) computer vision deep learning classification based on models architecture benchmarking, (2) ecological context retrieval, (3) Large Language Model (LLM) audit reasoning, and (4) interpretable output generation. Eight model architectures were evaluated across 169 species and 689,000 images. Among these, YOLOv26n-cls was selected as the deployment model, achieving  88.1\% on Top-1 scores and 98.4\% Top-5 accuracy, with an optimized inference speed of 4.8 ms CPU per image via TFLite export. An ablation study demonstrated that adding a Gemini LLM audit layer raised dangerous species safety recall from 36.1\% to 93.6\% for the deployed model. These results indicate that multimodal pipelines combining vision classification, ecological context, and LLM reasoning produce more informative and interpretable outputs compared to isolated image scoring and supporting more informed identification in real-word contexts. The coding section are available at GitHub and Live on Google Cloud.

\end{abstract}

\subsection{Background and Problem Statement}

\IEEEPARstart{M}{ushrooms} represent a growing area of scientific and commercial interest, with applications ranging from the food industry to bio pharmaceutical development\cite{mayirnao2025}. However, distinguishing between mushroom species remains difficult due to the fine-grained nature of their physical features between certain species\cite{sevindik2025}. 

In “real world” context, certain poisonous or edible mushroom species can appear nearly identical to the untrained eye, so that misidentifying them can therefore lead to fatal consequences. The challenge is further compounded by the fact that mushrooms appearance changes with age, weather exposure, and geographic condition, which is reducing the reliability of identification based solely on visual inspection\cite{picek2025fungitastic}.

Existing researches focuses on a range of architecture including convolutional neural networks, residual networks, and object detection models, demonstrating the promising prediction under controlled experimental settings\cite{kuznetsov2026}. Despite the growing availability of AI Deep leaning mushroom identification tools, several existing research papers recognized mushrooms as a standard image classification, predicting only species labels and a numerical confidence score from a single photograph.
 
 These outputs are generated without consideration of ecological factors such as habitat, season, or geographic location, even though these factors may be relevant when distinguishing between visually similar species and assessing the risk and supporting trust especially when safety is a critical challenge situation\cite{picek2025fungitastic}. Furthermore, current systems rarely provide language based reasoning to help users understand of the basics of a prediction, remain limited in practical capability and transparency and do not yet meet the border requirements of real word application and suitable for real-world use\cite{kuznetsov2026}.

To address these limitations, we propose a unified multimodal mushroom classification framework that integrates computer vision, structured ecological knowledge, and large language model reasoning as a complete end-to-end system, with the primary aim of reducing dangerous misidentification risk and providing interpretable identification support.

\subsection{\textbf{Research Questions}}
This study addresses the following four research questions corresponding directly to research gaps: 

\textbf{{RQ1:}} Which deep learning model architecture achieves the highest Top-1 and Top-5 classification accuracy for mushroom species identification across 169 species and what are the trade-offs in inference speed and deployment efficiency?

\textbf{{RQ2:}} To what extent do multimodal outputs, combining vision classification with ecological context and LLM reasoning, provide better identification information compared to single-image approaches for visually similar species?

\textbf{{RQ3:}} To what degree are LLM-generated species reasoning outputs consistent with structured ecological database records across a sample of classification decisions?

\textbf{{RQ4:}} Does a structured multimodal output schema provide demonstrably more identification-relevant information than a raw numerical confidence score?

\subsection{Research Contribution}
To address this gap, this study makes four original contributions: 

\subsubsection{A deployable multimodal pipeline.} We propose and implement a novel four stages pipeline that integrates the four components below into a single end-to0end deployable system.

\subsubsection{A systematic architecture benchmark.} We provide a systematic benchmark of YOLOv26n-cls, ConvNeXt-Tiny and DINOv2, evaluated across 169 species under production deployment conditions. 

\subsubsection{An LLM audit layer for visual verification.} The system applied an LLM audit layer that receives the input image, the vision prediction, and the ecological context record, and generate natural language reasoning for classification decisions, identify the prediction if it is plausible and the case where the vision model should not be trusted.

\subsubsection{An interpretable output schema.} We introduce output schema designed, including confidence levels, ecological notes, and identification guidance for real-word foraging contexts.

\section{\textbf{Related works}}
\subsection{Traditional Mushroom Identification}
Mushroom identification has evolved from traditional mushroom observation and expert mycological assessment to rule based systems, machine learning models and automated real time deep learning classifiers. These systems can now operate directly on raw visual input, but they remains limited in the safety critical context where implementing only visual classification may be insufficient \cite{kuznetsov2026}.

\subsection{Mushroom Species Classification Using Computer Vision}
As of 2026, computer vision has become the essential tools to classify mushroom species, particularity as deep learning method can learn directly from visual features such as color, shape, texture and structural patterns. Recent studies demonstrates the progression from CNN architectures toward more real time system, several study have compared DenseNet121,  EfficientNet-B0, MobileNetV3, and ShuffleNet for fungi classification\cite{korkmaz2025}\cite{ozsari2024}. This development has moved beyond static classification toward real time detection in including edge deployment and toxic species\cite{duman2025}\cite{wei2025pmyolo}.
 Among transfer and hybrid approaches Swin Transformer and a multimodal MDF-DETR architecture have been explored, though transfromer found underperfrom CNN baseline on smal fungi dataset\cite{ozsari2024}\cite{wang2025mdfdetr}. Base on this review, we sekect threee distint architecture for systemmatic benchmarking, YOLOv26n-cls, ConvNeXt-Tiny CNN arcgitecture and ConvNeXt-Tiny is self-supervised visin transformers.

\subsection{\textbf{Ecological and Contextual Factors}}
A significant challenge in mushroom classification is the lack of public datasets that combine visual data with ecological metadata such as habit, toxicity, or geographic context as classification inputs (Table I). Picek et al.\cite{picek2022danishfungi} introduced the Danish Fungi 2020 dataset (DF24-Mini split), an expert-verified dataset with temporal metadata across a wide range of species which is use in this study for external validation. Recently, Picek et al. \cite{picek2025fungitastic} furthers published baseline metrics for state-of-the-art architectures on this exact dataset. Their FungiTastic benchmark further shows that that visual classification alone achieves only 40\% F1 and remains unrealistic at taxonomic scale, providing strong motivation for integrating ecological context into the pipeline. 

\subsection{Explainability and Safety-Critical AI}
The black-box nature of deep learning is a particular challenge in safety-critical contexts, where users must not only receive a prediction but understand its basis. Within the mushroom literature, explainability has been applied through post-hoc visual heatmaps, including Grad-CAM and Score-CAM, Grad-CAM with Integrated Gradients\cite{sevindik2025}\cite{korkmaz2025}\cite{ozsari2024}. These technique may improve transparency, but a heatmap cannot explains why a prediction should be trusted, what alternatives were considered, or what action to take. Studies in toxic species detetcion still only predict toxic species with clas labels and bounding boxes with no data for safety guidance\cite{wei2025pmyolo}. While,  this limiation of miss classification between edible and toxic have been acknowledge in the litarature but do not include this into a decision support layer\cite{picek2025fungitastic}. 

\subsection{Summary of Gaps}
Despite recent advances in deep learning for automated mushroom species classification, several challenges remain unsolved, which can be summarized as four critical limitations. First, prior systems rely solely on single image inputs, which are insufficient to distinguish visually similar toxic and edible species that require multi angle or part based inspection  and therefore remain limited in practical capability[]. Second, as far as we know, non of the current literature integrates environmental context such as, season, habitat, geographic range and mycological habitat constraints into the model’s architecture pipeline, leaving predictions ecologically ungrounded. Third, most model performs like black-box systems that provides prediction scores without language explainable reasoning, leaving the users unable to assess why a prediction was made which may lead users to distrust the system[].

\section{\textbf{Methodology}}
\subsection{\textbf{Overview}}
Our methodology addresses the four research questions into two main components. The first components is supervised visual classification, several computer vision architecture were trained and evaluated on the same 169 species mushroom image dataset to establish baseline classification performance for deployment efficiency. In the second, we experimented on an ablation study to evaluate the safety contribution of an LLM audit layer when added to each vision model, aims to providing empirical evidence for the value of multimodal reasoning over unimodal confidence scoring. In the production pipeline, the selected deployment model YOLOv26n-cls generates a species prediction from input image, which is then passed through a four stage pipeline that retrieve ecological metadata from the structure database. This metadata is combined with user input data information such as location and season. The combined information is then passed to an LLM audit layer and a set of deterministic rules to produce the final output. 

\subsection{\textbf{Dataset}}

\subsubsection{\textbf{Image Dataset}}
All models were trained and evaluated on a dataset of 169 mushroom species comprising approximately 689,000 images sourced from Kaggle (zlatan599/mushroom1). Images were partitioned into an 80/10/10 train/validation/test split, with all models evaluated on the same held-out test set. The classification task was formulated as single-label supervised image classification, where each image is mapped to exactly one species label.

\subsubsection{\textbf{Ecological Database}}
To support multimodal pipeline, ecological metadata was compiled into a structured JSON database containing 12 fields per species, including toxicity classification, habitat, season, geographic region, morphological descriptions, known lookalike species, and foraging guidance. These records were compiled primarily from multiple open-source Wikipedia species articles and the iNaturalist open-access taxonomy database (iNaturalist, 2024), which were used to verify species names and ecological data. It is acknowledged that these records have not been independently verified against primary mycological sources and should therefore be treated as a prototype knowledge base. 


\subsection{\textbf{Vision Model Benchmarking}}
Eight computer vision architectures were initially explored to find thwe best model for production deployment. To keep the benchmark focused, three architecturally distinct models were selected for systematic benchmarking.

\textbf{YOLOv26n-cls} is a lightweight YOLO-based image classier pretrained on ImageNet 1k (Jocher et al., 2023). In this study, it was used as a whole image classifier that predicts one mushroom species and confidence score from each input image because of it is a small model size, fast inference for CPU, and suitability for lightweight deployment (Sapkota  Karkee, 2025).

\textbf{ConvNeXt-Tiny} is a modern convolutional neutrak network introduced by Liu et al.(2022). In this study, its original ImageNet classification head was replaced with a 169 class output layer for mushroom species classFification. It was included as a strong baseline for comparing accurcay agints the lightweight YOLO model. 

\textbf{DINOv2-Small} is a self supervised Vision Transformer introduced by Oquab et al. (2023). In this study, a custom classification head was added to adapt its pretrained visual representations to the 169 mushroom species. It was included to evaluate whether self-supervised transformer features improve generalisation for finegained mushroom classification. 


\subsection{\textbf{Experimental Configuration}}
All three benchmarked models were initialized from pretraind weights and fine tuned on 169 species using consistent evaluation protocols regardless of training hardware with input resolution of 244x244 and maximum of 50 epochs with early stopping (patience 10) and were trained locally on an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4070. 
Notably, YOLOv26n-cls was fully fine-tuned using SGD with cosine learning rate scheduling, and subsequently exported to TFLite float16. ConvNeXt-Tiny required a two-phase PEFT strategy due to its larger parameter count, with the backbone frozen in Phase 1 and unfrozen at a reduced learning rate in Phase 2 to prevent catastrophic forgetting. DINOv2-Small was fine-tuned using AdamW with differential learning rates (1e-05 backbone, 1e-04 head) and a 5-epoch warmup.

From all three models, YOLOv26n-cls was selected as the deployment model due to its balance between accuracy, model size, inference speed, and deployment suitability. A full configuration is provided in the Appendix:
Table~\ref{tab:output_schema} \label{text:schema_mention}


\subsection{\textbf{External Benchmarking using DF24-Mini}} 
As established in Section II, to ensure our system's visual feature-extraction capabilities generalize beyond our Kaggle training set, YOLOv26n-cls was additionally fine-tuned on the Danish Fungi (DF24-Mini) dataset  \cite{picek2022danishfungi} and evaluated against baselines published by Picek et al.[1]. 

The DF24-Mini dataset contains approximately 36,000 images across 182 species with only 11 species appear in both our primary training set and the DF24-Mini dataset (a $\sim$6\% overlap). This minimal overlap ensures the benchmark serves as a genuine test of morphological feature generalization rather than a re-test of familiar classes. The model was fine-tuned the YOLOv26n-cls model for 37 epochs at a 224$\times$224 resolution to maintain a strict 1:1 comparative methodology with the original Picek et al. baselines \cite{picek2022danishfungi}.

\subsection{\textbf{Multimodal Pipeline Design}}
The proposed framework is implemented as a four stages sequential pipeline, which each stage received the output from previous stages and generates additional contexts before passing the data forward. 

\textbf{1) Computer Vision Classification}.
The image is uploaded via HHTP POST to a FastAPI Vision API running YOLOv26n-cls in TFLite float16 format. The vision API returns the predicted species label and confidence score. If the confidence score falls below a threshold of 0.70, the pipeline will be notified as unreliable and stops the identification process. The prediction that above the confidence threshold are passed to the ecological retrieval stage.

\textbf{2) {Ecological Context Retrieval}}
The predicted species labels from stage 1 is then will look up against a structured ecological database using Pandas, unrecognized ecological fields are set to "Unknown". The retrieved ecological record, confidence score are then passed forward to stage 3, LLM audit layer. See appendix for data:

\textbf{3) {LLM Audit layers}}
The species label with confidence score, ecological database record including information provided by users (location, season) are assembled into structure prompt and submitted to the an  Open-source Google Gemini (production) or Llama 3 (local deployment).  The LLM is structured based on user's environment context and response with one of the three verdicts: plausible, suspicious or danger, with a short natural language explanation then passed to stage 4.

\textbf{4) {Output risk classification}}
To migrate this model hallucination, we set the python rule based pythons will follow the rule based with hard code, the rules is rule  (1) species with "deadly" or "highly toxic" keywords are unconditionally CRITICAL; (2) predictions below 0.70 confidence are HIGH; (3) an LLM suspicious verdict escalates to HIGH; (4) toxic but non-deadly species are MODERATE. This layered design ensures the LLM provides interpretive reasoning while deterministic rules provide safety guarantees that cannot be overridden by model uncertainty.



\subsection{\textbf{Deployment and Output}}  
The mushroom framework pipeline is implemented as two containerized microservices via Docker Compose for local deployment and Google Cloud Run for production. The vision API runs the TFLite float16 model in a lightweight container of approximately 200 MB, eliminating PyTorch runtime dependency. The UI microservice provides a  Gradio web interface. Both service connected over an internal Docker Network. The production on CPU constraints is primary reason for YOLOv26n-cls TFLite was selected as the deployment model over the higher accuracy alternatives. 

The final end-to-end pipeline produces a structured identification report delivered to the user via the Gradio web interface. The report is designed to provide non-technical users   containing the predicted species, confidence score, toxicity class, ecological context, LLM field assessment, risk level (CRITICAL/HIGH/MODERATE/LOW), and a plain-language recommendation, as defined in Table VI.Table. ~\ref{tab:output_schema} \label{text:schema_mention} 

\subsection{\textbf{Architecture Ablation Setup: Component Isolation}}
To rigorously evaluate the contribution of each component in our framework, we conducted a three-way ablation study. The goal was to demonstrate that neither component works effectively in isolation, and that our chosen combination (YOLOv26n-cls paired with Gemini) is the optimal configuration for mobile deployment.

We curated a specialized test set of 300 images (203 dangerous species, 97 safe species) using a fixed random seed (42) from the held-out test split. We evaluated three conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Unimodal Vision:} Relying strictly on the vision architectures alone (YOLOv26n-cls, ConvNeXt-Tiny, DINOv2-Small). A safety warning is triggered automatically only if the model's confidence falls below a 70\% threshold.
    \item \textbf{Unimodal LLM (Gemini Only):} Passing the raw image directly to the Gemini Flash Lite API to assess danger, with no vision model assistance.
    \item \textbf{Multimodal Pipeline:} The vision model identifies the species, retrieves ecological context, and Gemini performs a final safety audit.
\end{enumerate}

Performance was measured using three metrics: Safety Recall (percentage of dangerous mushrooms correctly flagged), False Positive Rate (percentage of safe mushrooms wrongly flagged as dangerous), and the F1 Score (the harmonic mean of precision and recall, serving as our primary balancing metric). Smaller local models (Llama 3.2 and Gemma 4) were initially tested but excluded due to fundamental reasoning failures at their parameter scales.

 \subsection{\textbf{Multimodal vs Unimodal Pipeline (RQ2)}}

The ablation study yielded several critical findings regarding system safety and component synergy, detailed in Table \ref{tab:ablation_full_results}.

\textbf{Neither component works well alone.} Vision models on their own missed between 70\% and 85\% of dangerous species. Conversely, while the Gemini LLM operating alone achieved a high safety recall of 92.6\%, it incorrectly flagged 70.7\% of safe species as dangerous. In practice, this level of false alarms would condition users to ignore the warnings entirely, rendering the standalone LLM impractical. The combination of both components is what works.

\textbf{Vision model accuracy does not equal fallback safety.} If network connectivity fails, the application must fall back to the vision model alone. Under these unimodal conditions, the highly accurate ConvNeXt and DINOv2 models are the most dangerous fallback options, catching only 14.3\% and 15.3\% of dangerous species, respectively. This is caused by an overconfidence trap: they almost always generate a high-confidence prediction, meaning our automatic low-confidence warning (<70\%) rarely triggers. YOLOv26n-cls, being less certain on average, triggers the safety threshold more frequently, catching 30.0\% of dangerous species on its own. A highly accurate but overconfident model is the most dangerous fallback because it provides the user with a confident wrong answer and no warning.

\textbf{Synergy normalizes performance.} Once the Gemini auditor is introduced, the safety gap between the vision architectures closes. All three Multimodal configurations achieved F1 scores within an exceptionally tight margin (80.0\% to 81.9\%). Adding Gemini effectively bridges the gap between a lightweight, inexpensive model and a massive, computationally expensive one.

\section{LLM Audit Prompt (Ollama/Gemini)}

The following prompt was used for the LLM audit layer alongside each mushroom photograph during evaluation.

\textbf{System Prompt:} You are a field mycology safety auditor. You have been given a photo of a mushroom taken in the field. A computer vision model identified it as [PREDICTED SPECIES] with [CONFIDENCE]\% confidence.

Ecological database entry for [PREDICTED SPECIES]:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Toxicity: [TOXICITY]
    \item Edibility: [EDIBILITY]
    \item Typical habitat: [HABITAT]
    \item Season: [SEASON]
    \item Region: [REGION]
    \item Key warnings: [KEY WARNINGS]
\end{itemize}

Visual identification features to check in the photo:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Cap: [CAP DESCRIPTION]
    \item Gills: [GILL DESCRIPTION]
    \item Stem: [STEM DESCRIPTION]
    \item Smell: [SMELL]
    \item Dangerous lookalikes: [LOOKALIKES]
\end{itemize}

Your Task:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Look at the photo carefully and compare it against the known features above.
    \item Comment on what you can see --- cap shape/colour, gill colour, stem, any bruising or staining.
    \item Mention relevant field checks the user should do in person.
    \item State AGREE if the photo is consistent with [PREDICTED SPECIES], or DISAGREE if something looks wrong.
    \item If the species is toxic or deadly, issue a clear safety warning.
\end{enumerate}

Begin your response with "AGREE", "DISAGREE", or "DANGER", then give your field assessment.

\textit{Note: The ecological fields (habitat, season, toxicity, morphological features) are drawn from a curated species context database of 169 mushroom classes. In the ablation evaluation, user environment fields (season, location) were set to Unknown as the test set is evaluated without real-world geolocation context.}

\textit{Why three architectures were selected for systematic
benchmarking.} The three benchmarked models cover three distinct
representation paradigms: (i)~YOLOv26n-cls --- detection-derived
classifier, ImageNet-1k pretrained, optimised for edge inference;
(ii)~ConvNeXt-Tiny --- modern CNN with ViT-style design choices,
ImageNet-1k pretrained, larger parameter budget; (iii)~DINOv2-Small ---
self-supervised ViT pretrained on 142M images without labels, intended
to test whether self-supervised features generalise to fine-grained
mycological classification. EfficientNet-B0 and the YOLO baselines were
trained to set context but not subjected to the full multimodal
ablation; TaxonomicYOLO26 was an exploratory dual-head experiment
(predicting genus and species from a shared backbone) which
underperformed the single-head YOLOv26n by 8.4\,pp Top-1 and was
abandoned. ViT-S/16 was scoped but not trained to convergence within
project budget; YOLOv11n-cls was bypassed in favour of the newer
YOLOv26n.
 
\textit{Architecture-specific training notes.} ConvNeXt-Tiny full
fine-tuning of all 28.6\,M parameters required approximately
7,278\,seconds per epoch on the RTX~4070, making 50-epoch training
computationally infeasible. A two-phase Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning
(PEFT) strategy reduced per-epoch time to $\sim$776\,seconds (a
$9.4\times$ speedup) by freezing the stem and backbone stages 1--3
during Phase~1 (epochs 1--10), then unfreezing the backbone with a
differential learning rate (4e-05 backbone vs 4e-04 head) for Phase~2
(epochs 11--50). DINOv2-Small used differential learning rates from the
start (1e-05 backbone, 1e-04 head, a $10\times$ ratio) to preserve its
self-supervised representations during fine-tuning, with a 5-epoch
warmup followed by cosine decay; training terminated at epoch~49.
YOLOv26n-cls used full fine-tuning across all parameters for 50 epochs
under standard cosine LR scheduling and was subsequently exported to
TFLite float16 via the XNNPACK delegate, reducing the Docker image from
$\sim$1.5\,GB to $\sim$200\,MB while preserving prediction outputs.
 
\textit{Hardware caveat.} DINOv2-Small was trained on an
NVIDIA~A100-SXM4-40GB via Google Colab; YOLOv26n-cls and ConvNeXt-Tiny
were trained locally on an NVIDIA GeForce RTX~4070. Training-time
comparisons should therefore be interpreted with caution, but accuracy
metrics are directly comparable as all models were evaluated on the
same held-out test split.
 
\textit{TaxonomicYOLO26 — abandoned dual-head experiment.} The
TaxonomicYOLO26 variant added a parallel genus head to the YOLOv26n
backbone, with the intention that hierarchical supervision (predicting
both genus and species) would improve fine-grained discrimination on
visually similar within-genus species (e.g.~\textit{Amanita phalloides}
vs \textit{Amanita citrina}). In practice, the shared backbone splitting
its capacity between genus and species objectives reduced single-head
species accuracy by 8.4\,pp (79.7\% vs 88.1\% for vanilla YOLOv26n). The
experiment was discontinued; future work could revisit dual-head
training with a per-task adapter rather than a fully shared backbone.
 
 
\section{LLM Ablation Study Details}\label{app:ablation}
 
The ablation study referenced in Sections~\ref{subsec:multimodal}
and~\ref{subsec:llm} evaluated three vision models (YOLOv26n-cls,
ConvNeXt-Tiny~PEFT, DINOv2-Small) crossed with three LLM
configurations (none, Gemma~4 2B, Gemini~3~Flash). Two configurations
were excluded from the final reported numbers: a Llama3-based
configuration was excluded for label-echo behaviour described below,
and Gemma~4~2B was excluded after empirical failure also described
below. Final reported numbers use \textbf{Gemini~3~Flash} for the LLM
audit layer.
 
\paragraph{Test set construction.} The evaluation was restricted to the
40 dangerous species listed in Appendix~\ref{app:species},
Table~\ref{tab:dangerous-species}, sampled at up to 20 images per class
from the held-out test split (\texttt{data/dataset\_split/test/}) at
seed~42. The full sampled pool yields up to 800 images; the partial run
reported in this paper covers $n=245$ images. A full 800-image run is
identified as future work, particularly for stable estimates of
high-confidence misclassification recall (Metric~2).
 
\paragraph{Vision-model inference latency in the ablation harness.}
On the dangerous-species ablation harness (CPU, batch=1), median
per-image vision inference times were 15.3\,ms (YOLOv26n-cls),
30.1\,ms (ConvNeXt-Tiny), and 38.7\,ms (DINOv2-Small). These ablation
latencies are higher than the deployment-grade timings in
Table~\ref{tab:full-leaderboard} (e.g.~4.8\,ms for the TFLite f16
build) because they were measured on the un-exported PyTorch graph
inside the ablation runner rather than on the production TFLite path.
Both numbers are reported faithfully in their respective contexts.
 
\paragraph{Llama3 (text-only) — label-echo failure mode.} An initial
Llama3-based configuration produced an artificially inflated
$\sim$90\% safety recall in early testing. Inspection of the model's
verdicts revealed that the audit prompt embeds the toxicity field from
the context CSV verbatim (e.g.~\texttt{Toxicity:~Deadly}) and instructs
the LLM to issue a safety warning when a species is toxic. Llama3
re-read the embedded label and echoed it back as the verdict --- this
is database-label lookup, not visual or ecological reasoning. The
model never genuinely engaged with the image content. Llama3 was
excluded from the final ablation on this basis. As a secondary issue,
text-only ecological reasoning requires real location/season metadata;
the ablation harness uses \texttt{Unknown}/\texttt{Unknown} for these
fields, removing the only signal a text-only LLM could meaningfully
use.
 
\paragraph{Gemma~4 2B (multimodal) — capability failure mode.} An
initial \texttt{image\_path} forwarding bug caused Gemma to default to
\textsc{Agree} on every case (the model never saw the images). After
the bug was fixed, Gemma's safety recall converged to a value
statistically indistinguishable from the no-LLM baseline. We attribute
this to insufficient world knowledge at the 2B-parameter scale: Gemma's
verbal reasoning was fluent but did not reliably cross-reference visual
features against the database content for fine-grained mycological
discrimination. Gemma was excluded from the final ablation on this
basis.
 
\paragraph{Gemini~3~Flash (deployed configuration).} The deployed
configuration uses Gemini~3~Flash via the Google API. Production
deployment is in code as
\texttt{gemini-3-flash-preview}\footnote{See
\texttt{config.py}: \texttt{GEMINI\_MODEL = "gemini-3-flash-preview"}.}.
Median per-request latency is $\sim$500--1500\,ms (vendor-reported);
this dominates total pipeline latency relative to vision inference.
 
\paragraph{Manual coding protocol for RQ3.} To produce a measured
LLM-reasoning-consistency rate to fill the \texttt{[TODO]} in
Section~\ref{subsec:llm}, sample $N=30$ Gemini audit responses
uniformly at random from the 245-image evaluation. For each response,
code three binary criteria:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Does the response correctly cite the species' ecological context
      from the database (toxicity, habitat, season, region)?
\item Does the response reference visual features visible in the image
      (cap shape/colour, gills, stem, bruising)?
\item When the LLM disagrees with the vision model, does it justify the
      disagreement against database content or visual evidence (rather
      than asserting it without reason)?
\end{enumerate}
Report agreement rates as $X/30$, $Y/30$, and $Z/Z_\mathrm{disagree}$.
Cohen's $\kappa$ may be computed if two coders rate independently.
 
\paragraph{Limitations.} The headline numbers in
Table~\ref{tab:multimodal} are from $n=245$ rather than the full
$n=800$. Cell counts for high-confidence misclassification recall are
small enough (1--7 cases per model) that those values should be read as
existence-of-effect evidence rather than precise estimates. A full
800-image rerun is identified as future work, alongside per-class
recall analysis once the cell counts are sufficient.

\subsection{Multimodal vs Unimodal Pipeline (RQ2)}\label{subsec:multimodal}
 
To quantify the safety contribution of the LLM audit layer, the pipeline
was evaluated in two configurations on a stratified sample of $n=245$
images from 40 dangerous-species classes (Appendix~\ref{app:species},
Table~\ref{tab:dangerous-species}; sampling protocol seed~42, $\leq 20$
images/class):
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Vision-only}: vision model + 70\% confidence-threshold
      rule. Predictions below 0.70 confidence auto-flag as
      \textsc{High}; predictions at or above 0.70 are accepted as the
      species output.
\item \textbf{Full pipeline}: vision model + ecological retrieval +
      Gemini~3 Flash audit + risk engine.
\end{itemize}
The primary metric is \textit{safety recall}: the fraction of
dangerous-species images correctly flagged at \textsc{High} or
\textsc{Critical} risk.
 
\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Safety recall across pipeline configurations on the
dangerous-species ablation set ($n=245$, 40 classes, seed 42).}
\label{tab:multimodal}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lccc@{}}
\hline
\textbf{Vision Model}  & \textbf{Vision-only} & \textbf{+\,Gemini} & \textbf{Lift} \\
\hline
YOLOv26n-cls           & 36.1\%  & \textbf{93.6\%} & +57.5\,pp \\
ConvNeXt-Tiny (PEFT)   & 20.8\%  & 90.1\%          & +69.3\,pp \\
DINOv2-Small           & 23.8\%  & 90.1\%          & +66.3\,pp \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}
 
Three findings emerge.
 
\textit{First}, the multimodal pipeline produces a +57--69\,pp absolute
improvement in safety recall regardless of vision model. Removing the
LLM audit collapses recall by 60--70\,pp uniformly --- the LLM is the
primary safety mechanism in the pipeline, not an optional add-on.
 
\textit{Second}, the more accurate classifiers (ConvNeXt-Tiny and
DINOv2-Small) are the most dangerous configurations \emph{without} an
LLM. Their lower vision-only recall (20.8\%, 23.8\%) reflects systematic
overconfidence: they almost always predict above the 70\%
confidence-threshold, so the threshold rule rarely fires as a safety
net. YOLOv26n-cls's lower confidence on fine-grained predictions trips
the threshold rule more often, giving it higher vision-only recall
(36.1\%) despite being the less accurate classifier. In a pipeline
without language-based reasoning, an accurate but overconfident model
is therefore less safe than a less accurate but better-calibrated one.
 
\textit{Third}, the LLM lift is largest for the most overconfident
models (+69.3\,pp ConvNeXt, +66.3\,pp DINOv2): the LLM is doing more
work where the threshold rule does less.
 
To isolate the LLM's visual contribution from the threshold rule, we
report a secondary metric: \textit{high-confidence misclassification
recall} --- cases where the vision model predicted a safe species at
$\geq 70$\% confidence on a dangerous true class but the LLM correctly
escalated. Vision-only recall on this metric is 0\% by construction.
Gemini achieved 85.7\% (6/7) for YOLOv26n-cls and 100\% (1/1) for
ConvNeXt-Tiny; DINOv2-Small produced zero such cases on the 245-image
sample. Cell counts are small because high-confidence dangerous
misidentifications are rare ($<3$\% of the sample). Per-model and
per-class breakdowns of both metrics are in
Appendix~\ref{app:ablation}.
 

\subsection{Baseline Vision Model Benchmarking}
Following a review of related work, eight models architectures were initially experimented for evaluation on Github Repository. Due to unnecessary to report all here, three architecturally distinct models were selected for systematic benchmarking: YOLOv26n-cls, ConvNeXt-Tiny, and DINOv2-Small. These three models represent fundamentally different architecture, which is useful when compare the accuracy, inference speed, and deployment suitability across paradigms. After benchmarking, this selected model is integrated into the purpose multimodel pipeline. 

\textbf{YOLOv26n-cls} is a image classification variant of YOLO26 (You Only Look Once) by Ultralytics in early 2026 (Jocher et al., 2023). It is specifically optimized for tasks where an entire image is assigned a single label and confidence score and adopted from the object detection framework by replacing the detection head with a lightweight classification head. The nano variant included minimal parameters count of 1.74 m and was pretrained on  ImageNet 1k. This make the YOLO lightweight and good for production [].

\textbf{ConvNeXt-Tiny }is purely convolutional network intriduced by  Liu et al. (2022), designed by systematically applying design principles from a Vision Transformer Literature to a standard residual network. Key architectural changes include 7×7 depthwise convolutions for a larger per-layer receptive field, an inverted bottleneck structure, LayerNorm in place of BatchNorm, and GELU activations. These changes produce richer spatial feature representations than standard CNNs, with a total parameter count of 28.6M. Pre-trained weights on ImageNet-1k were loaded via torchvision, with the original 1000-class head replaced by a custom head comprising LayerNorm, AdaptiveAvgPool2d, Flatten, Dropout(0.3), and a Linear(768, 169) output layer.

\textbf{DINOv2-Small} is a self-supervised Vision Transformer introduced by Oquab et al. (2023), pre-trained without labels using a self-distillation objective on a curated dataset of 142 million images. Unlike supervised transformers, DINOv2 produces general-purpose visual features that transfer effectively across domains. The ViT-S/14 variant was selected, comprising 22M parameters with a patch size of 14×14 pixels. Pre-trained weights were loaded from the official facebook research hub with a custom classification head appended for the 169 species class. 

\subsection{\textbf{Vision Model Training}}

\subsubsection{Training Conditions}
All models were trained on the same 169 species dataset using the same 80/10/10 train/validation/test split. It is acknowledge that DINOv2 was trained on an NIVIDIA  A100-SXM4-40GB via Google Colab, while YOLOv26n-cls and ConvNeXt-Tiny were trained locally on an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4070. Training time comparisons across models should therefore be interpreted with cautions as they reflect different hardware conditions. Accuracy and metrics remains incomparable as they are evaluated on the same test set regarding less of trainings hardware.

\textbf{YOLOv26n-cls} was trained using full fine-tuning across all parameters for 50 epochs with cosine learning rate scheduling and early stopping patience of 10. Following training, the model was exported to TFLite float16 format via the XNNPACK delegate, reducing the Docker image from approximately 1.5 GB to 200 MB while maintaining identical prediction output.

\textbf{ConvNeXt-Tiny} required Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) due to its larger parameter count. Full fine-tuning of all 28.6M parameters produced epoch times of approximately 7,278 seconds on the RTX 4070, making 50-epoch training computationally infeasible. A two-phase strategy was adopted: in Phase 1 (epochs 1--10), backbone stages 1--3, the stem, and all downsampling layers were frozen, reducing per-epoch time to approximately 776 seconds — a speedup of 9.4×. In Phase 2 (epochs 11--50), the full backbone was unfrozen with a backbone learning rate of 4e-05 to prevent catastrophic forgetting, while the classifier head retained its Phase 1 rate of 4e-04. Gradient clipping (max\_norm = 1.0) and CosineAnnealingLR were applied throughout both phases.

\textbf{DINOv2-Small } was trained using AdamW with differential learning rates: a backbone rate of 1e-05 and a head rate of 1e-04, implementing a 10× differential to preserve the self-supervised representations during fine-tuning. A warmup phase of 5 epochs preceded cosine decay over the remaining 45 epochs, with a maximum of 50 epochs and early stopping patience of 10. Training terminated at epoch 49.
